# Fibonacci Boundary Architecture and Variational Observer Selection: A Conjecture at the FEP Interface

**Peter Viviani**  
Independent Researcher

**Status:** technical preprint for external review

## Abstract

The theorem-grade mathematical core of the Golden Horizon Principle (GHP) is the claim that, within a stated class of braiding-universal unitary modular tensor category architectures, Fibonacci is the minimal non-abelian candidate. The physical conjecture is separate: can admissible observer-boundary architectures be selected by a variational principle on a space of topological boundary models, with the Fibonacci architecture as the minimum of a suitable information-cost functional? This paper frames that conjecture at the interface with the Free Energy Principle (FEP), using Markov blankets, variational free energy, and finite observer access as the main vocabulary. The central open problem is the missing write-law: a derived rule by which a finite boundary turns continuous dynamics into discrete records. No philosophy, consciousness claims, or GR unification claims are advanced in the main text. A GR-side appendix is included only as quarantined context for colleagues interested in a separate metric-level bridge target.

## 1. The Mathematical Spine

**Status:** theorem-grade within a stated mathematical domain

Let \(\mathcal{C}\) be a unitary modular tensor category with at least one non-invertible simple object and with braiding-universal computational power in the sense of topological quantum computation. Let

\[
D^2(\mathcal{C}) = \sum_i d_i^2
\]

denote total quantum dimension.

The core mathematical proposition used by GHP is the following:

> **Minimality proposition.** Within the stated class of braiding-universal unitary modular tensor category architectures, Fibonacci is the minimal non-abelian candidate by total quantum dimension.

**Claim label:** theorem-grade within the stated categorical class

The Fibonacci category has two simple objects, \(1\) and \(\tau\), with fusion rule

\[
\tau \otimes \tau = 1 \oplus \tau.
\]

Taking quantum dimensions gives

\[
d_\tau^2 = 1 + d_\tau,
\]

whose unique positive solution is

\[
d_\tau = \phi = \frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2}.
\]

Therefore

\[
D^2 = 1 + d_\tau^2 = 1 + \phi^2 = 2 + \phi.
\]

This is not an isolated numerical appearance of \(\phi\). It is the algebraic consequence of a concrete fusion rule. The relevant mathematical lane is established by several independent sources. Freedman, Larsen, and Wang show that Fibonacci anyons support universal quantum computation by braiding alone. Ostrik classifies rank-2 fusion categories, giving the clean low-rank categorical setting for the Fibonacci lane. Rowell, Stong, and Wang classify modular tensor categories in low rank and isolate Fibonacci as a central low-rank example in the unitary modular setting. Edie-Michell classifies unitary fusion categories tensor-generated by an object of dimension \((1+\sqrt{5})/2\), hardening the \(\phi\)-dimension lane directly. Jones provides the canonical subfactor-index background for the appearance of \(\phi^2\) in finite-depth inclusion theory.

Taken together, these references do not prove a physical selection principle. They do something narrower and cleaner: they make the categorical lane precise. Fibonacci enters here through standard fusion/category machinery rather than through numerical pattern matching.

For the present paper, the relevant mathematical moral is that the Fibonacci category is admissible, minimal, and computationally nontrivial in an established sense. It is therefore a legitimate boundary-architecture candidate if one later wishes to formulate a physical or inferential selection principle over an admissible model class. The mathematics justifies taking Fibonacci seriously as a candidate architecture; it does not justify assuming that this architecture is realized in nature.

**Scope Limit**

This section proves minimality only inside the stated categorical class. It does **not** prove that nature selects Fibonacci, that observer-boundaries are unitary modular tensor categories, or that categorical minimality by itself has physical force.

## 2. The Architecture / Dynamics Split

**Status:** verified-computation plus established adjacent condensed-matter literature

The strongest current computational lesson supporting GHP is the architecture / dynamics split seen in the golden chain of interacting Fibonacci anyons. In the antiferromagnetic Fibonacci chain, Feiguin et al. showed that the model is critical and described by a two-dimensional conformal field theory with central charge

\[
c = \frac{7}{10},
\]

namely the tricritical Ising universality class. Later work by Pfeifer et al. sharpened the protection and symmetry structure of criticality in chains of interacting anyons.

The point for GHP is specific. Fibonacci structure governs the topological information architecture, while the low-energy dynamical universality class is tricritical Ising rather than "golden" in any naive numerical sense. This is exactly the separation needed to block numerological overreach.

The relevant lesson can be stated plainly:

1. The architecture is Fibonacci.
2. The residual low-energy dynamics are tricritical Ising.
3. Therefore, architectural invariants and dynamical universality classes need not coincide.

This gives the cleanest current reason not to read GHP as a claim that \(\phi\) should pervade all observables. If there is a viable physical version of GHP, it is more plausibly an architecture-selection statement than a statement about generic dynamical ratios.

**Claim label:** verified-computation

The role of this section is defensive as much as constructive. It rules out an easy but damaging misreading: that the appearance of \(\phi\) at the categorical level licenses a search for golden-ratio observables throughout low-energy phenomenology. The golden-chain result indicates the opposite. One may have a Fibonacci topological architecture whose continuum infrared description belongs to a standard non-Fibonacci universality class. This is precisely the kind of separation one would expect if architecture and dynamics enter a theory at different levels.

For the FEP audience, the analogy is straightforward. A generative architecture and an effective dynamical regime need not be coded by the same invariants. One can therefore ask whether an observer-boundary architecture is selected for inferential reasons without assuming that its low-energy or coarse-grained phenomenology should visibly reproduce the same algebraic signatures.

## 3. Observer-Boundary as Variational Problem

**Status:** candidate conjecture

The central conjecture can be posed in a form that is natural at the Free Energy Principle interface.

Suppose that an observer-boundary is not characterized first by its substrate, but by the way it separates internal, external, sensory, and active states. In FEP language, this is the Markov-blanket partition. The blanket defines the finite interface through which a system can register, act upon, and infer hidden external structure. Friston's "Life as we know it," Kirchhoff-Parr-Palacios-Friston-Kiverstein on Markov blankets, Friston's "A free energy principle for a particular physics," Parr-Friston on generalised free energy, and Da Costa-Friston-Heins-Pavliotis on Bayesian mechanics all sharpen different parts of this interface story.

The question raised by GHP is not whether FEP already implies Fibonacci. It does not. The question is whether the FEP framework leaves room for a topological-sector selection problem of the following kind:

> among admissible observer-boundary architectures, does a free-energy-like or information-cost-like variational principle prefer the minimal architecture capable of non-abelian, self-referential, computationally universal closure?

GHP proposes total quantum dimension,

\[
D^2,
\]

as the first candidate topological information-cost functional. The thought is not that \(D^2\) is already free energy, nor that it is already the correct functional. The thought is that it may play the role of a topological complexity cost on the space of admissible boundary architectures.

**Claim label:** candidate

More concretely, the conjecture asks whether a boundary that must simultaneously support finite inferential closure, nontrivial compositional structure, and robust separation of internal from external states is subject to a constrained architecture-selection problem. In ordinary active-inference settings, one optimizes states, beliefs, or policies under a variational objective. Here the proposal moves one level higher: the object under comparison is not only a state-estimate but an admissible class of boundary architectures.

This suggests a variational problem of the following form:

\[
\mathcal{A}_* = \arg\min_{\mathcal{A} \in \mathfrak{A}_{\mathrm{adm}}} \mathcal{I}(\mathcal{A}),
\]

where:

- \(\mathfrak{A}_{\mathrm{adm}}\) is a space of admissible boundary architectures,
- \(\mathcal{A}\) is a candidate architecture,
- \(\mathcal{I}\) is a yet-to-be-derived information-cost functional,
- \(D^2(\mathcal{A})\) is the leading candidate for the topological term in \(\mathcal{I}\).

The conjectural claim is then:

> If admissibility includes non-abelianity, braided universality, and finite self-referential closure, a free-energy-like minimization on \(\mathfrak{A}_{\mathrm{adm}}\) may select the Fibonacci architecture as the first stable minimum.

That claim is open. Nothing in the current mathematics establishes it. The value of the conjecture is that it turns "Fibonacci may matter" into a concrete selection problem rather than a vague analogy.

This is where FEP becomes relevant. If the blanket is the interface across which inference is implemented, then architecture matters. Not every admissible interface need be equally efficient, equally stable, or equally compressible. The FEP side contributes the language of variational selection and finite inferential boundaries. The categorical side contributes a sharply defined space of topological candidates. The missing bridge is the law that connects the two.

One possible way to make the conjecture nontrivial is to regard total quantum dimension as only the leading topological term in a richer information functional,

\[
\mathcal{I}(\mathcal{A}) = \alpha D^2(\mathcal{A}) + \beta \,\mathcal{R}(\mathcal{A}) + \gamma \,\mathcal{S}(\mathcal{A}) + \cdots,
\]

where \(\mathcal{R}\) could represent a robustness or recoverability penalty and \(\mathcal{S}\) a stability or separability term. No such functional is derived here. The point is only that the conjecture can be posed in a mathematically disciplined way: admissible architectures form the domain, and a variational principle acts on that domain.

**Open problem:** does existing FEP formalism already admit an architecture-level objective of this kind, or would such a construction require a genuine extension beyond current state-space and policy-space formulations?

## 4. The Missing Write-Law

**Status:** open problem with bridge-object candidate

The central open problem is the write-law.

The categorical lane says something about admissible architecture. The FEP lane says something about bounded inference. What is missing is a derived rule for how a finite observer-boundary turns continuous dynamics into discrete records.

One candidate formalization is a time-indexed conditional expectation

\[
E_t : M_t \to N_t,
\]

where:

- \(M_t\) is an ambient algebra of possible distinctions,
- \(N_t\) is the observer-accessible subalgebra,
- \(E_t\) is a lawful finite-access projection.

In this reading, \(E_t\) is not "forgetting" in the loose psychological sense. It is structured accessibility: the map from what is available in principle to what is writable for a bounded observer.

**Claim label:** bridge-object candidate

To obtain a write-law, however, one would need more than finite access. One would need thresholded or dissipative behavior that yields discrete events such as:

- `WRITE`: durable update,
- `WITNESS`: temporary live retention without durable inscription,
- `RELEASE`: demotion or loss of durable accessibility.

Formally, the problem is whether a continuous or stochastic underlying flow can be coupled to a thresholded family of projections so that discrete update events emerge from lawful finite access rather than being inserted by hand.

At the level of mathematical form, one is asking for a family \(\{E_t\}_{t \ge 0}\) and an event rule \(\Theta\) such that

\[
\Theta(E_t, \rho_t, \eta_t) \in \{\texttt{WRITE}, \texttt{WITNESS}, \texttt{RELEASE}\},
\]

with \(\rho_t\) denoting the relevant state and \(\eta_t\) denoting threshold or dissipation parameters. The point of this notation is not to pretend that the rule is known; it is to make clear what a successful derivation would have to produce. A write-law worthy of the name must specify the transition from continuous availability to discrete record formation without appealing to an extraneous observer outside the model.

Kosaki is relevant because it extends Jones-style index theory and conditional expectation structure to arbitrary factors, giving a precise algebraic setting for finite-access projection. Fewster is relevant because the split property in locally covariant QFT gives a rigorous route for treating local regions as subsystems in curved spacetime. Neither result supplies a GHP write-law. They only show that the finite-access route is mathematically serious and not merely metaphorical.

The open question can be stated directly:

> Is a time-indexed conditional expectation, equipped with thresholded or dissipative structure, a well-posed way to formulate a boundary write-law within or adjacent to current FEP formalism?

This is the point on which feedback from the FEP group would be most valuable. If the question is ill-posed, that is immediately useful. If it is well-posed, then the next step is to determine whether existing FEP mathematics already contains a structure that could implement it.

This is also the point at which GHP is most incomplete. Without a write-law, the framework remains a boundary-selection conjecture with a mathematically clean architecture lane and an unbuilt dynamical bridge. That limitation is structural, not cosmetic, and should be stated as such.

## 5. Three Questions for the FEP Group

**Status:** open questions

1. Is the boundary write-law problem, formulated as a thresholded family of conditional expectations \(E_t : M_t \to N_t\) producing discrete write-events, well-posed within current FEP or active-inference formalism?

2. Does existing FEP formalism already contain a route to topological-sector selection that could, in principle, accommodate minimization over admissible UMTC architectures rather than only over state estimates or policy spaces?

3. Who in the UCL / FEP orbit is working most directly on the formal boundary-machinery side, especially operator-algebraic, information-geometric, or Bayesian-mechanics approaches relevant to finite-access subsystem structure?

## APPENDIX A -- FOR GR COLLEAGUES ONLY; NOT PART OF THE CORE FEP CLAIM

**Status:** verified-computation; strictly quarantined from the main conjecture

In Schwarzschild geometry, the Viviani Phi Surface (VPS) is defined by the static-observer fixed-point condition

\[
\gamma(r) = \frac{r}{r_s},
\]

which yields

\[
r = \phi r_s.
\]

This is a verified metric-level computation. It is not proof that nature selects Fibonacci. It is not proof that GHP recovers GR. It is not a horizon in the technical GR sense: it is not a null surface, not a Killing horizon, and not a trapped surface.

The reason it remains of possible interest is narrow. The same quadratic

\[
x^2 = x + 1
\]

appears both in the Fibonacci quantum-dimension equation and in the VPS fixed-point condition. At present this is only algebraic resonance, not structural identity.

Prior-art context matters. Cruz, Olivares, and Villanueva identify a golden-ratio relation in null-geodesic turning points for Schwarzschild-Kottler black holes. Coelho and Herdeiro identify a golden-ratio critical structure in optical geometry for the relativistic Euler three-body problem involving black holes. Hod proves upper bounds on photonsphere radii. These results show that golden-ratio and distinguished-radius structures are not alien to black-hole geometry. They do not validate the VPS interpretation or the GHP framework.

This appendix is included only for colleagues interested in GR-side bridge targets. It does not support the FEP-side conjecture advanced in the main text.
